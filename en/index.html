Questions

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between SAC and Price?

In SAC, regular amortization is calculated from the remaining balance and term, and payments generally decrease over the financing period. In Price, financial installments are calculated to remain regular under the conditions considered, with increasing amortization and decreasing interest. Monetary correction and extra payments can change these behaviors.

Does an extra amortization reduce the term or the installment amount?

It depends on the selected goal. When reducing the term, the simulator recalculates the remaining financing duration. When reducing the installment amount, it preserves the term and recalculates future payments. The financial institution may apply different contractual rules.

What is monetary correction of the outstanding balance?

It is the update of the balance by a percentage or index before monthly interest and amortization are calculated. The correction increases the nominal balance and affects subsequent values, but it is not treated as a separate monthly fee.

Which TR rate does the simulator use?

The suggested value is the highest monthly TR found in the latest 12 months available in the local file updated by the project. This rate is applied as a fixed monthly projection. The actual TR may change every month.

Is Selic the financing rate?

No. The Selic target rate is used only as an initial reference when there is no rate saved by the user. The rate actually charged on financing depends on the institution, contract, customer profile, and transaction conditions.

Why can the result differ from the bank calculation?

Financial institutions may consider exact dates, daily periods, variable insurance, fees, taxes, rounding, specific amortization rules, updated indexes, and other elements of the total effective cost. The simulator works with the assumptions entered by the user.

Does this simulation have legal or contractual validity?

No. The results are estimates only and do not represent a credit proposal, approval, binding offer, financial recommendation, or official terms from an institution. Review the contract and the total effective cost before making a decision.

Can I simulate the use of FGTS as an extra amortization?

Yes. The amount can be entered as an extra amortization in the estimated application month, with the goal of reducing the term or installments. The simulator does not verify eligibility, limits, usage intervals, or other FGTS or contract rules.

What is the difference between a nominal and an effective annual rate?

A nominal annual rate is divided by 12 to obtain the monthly rate. An effective annual rate is converted to the equivalent monthly rate through compound capitalization. Use the rate type stated in the contract so the simulation applies the correct conversion.

Important: This tool provides only an estimated simulation, with no legal, contractual, or financial validity. Results depend exclusively on the data entered and the assumptions used, such as fixed rate, monthly periods, projected monetary correction, and extra costs filled in by the user. The simulation does not represent a credit proposal, approval, binding offer, financial recommendation, legal advice, or the actual terms practiced by financial institutions. To contract or compare a real credit operation, consult the agreement, total effective cost, taxes, fees, insurance, correction indexes, and all official terms provided by the responsible institution.

Amortization systems

SAC

In SAC, base amortization is calculated from the remaining balance and term. When an extra amortization reduces term, the simulator recalculates the remaining term while trying to approximately preserve the reference financial payment. When it reduces payment, it preserves the term and recalculates amortization.

Price

In Price, regular financial installments are constant for the rate and balance considered in the recalculation. Amortization starts lower and gradually increases while interest tends to fall.

Extra amortizations and monetary correction can change balance and payment evolution in both systems.
